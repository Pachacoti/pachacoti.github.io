My Research



Currently I mainly focus on small bodies in the solar system, in particular comets and asteroids. These objects are conceived to be remnants and leftovers from the early solar system, and therefore may carry information of great importance and scientific interest about the early stage and evolution of the solar system. The targets on which I have spent the most time can be conveniently categorised into the following classes, i.e. active asteroids, near-Sun comets, ultra-distant comets, and Trojan asteroids. Besides this list, interstellar objects will also become the targets of my research focus whenever they are discovered.


Active Asteroids

Active asteroids are dynamically asteroidal bodies (\(T_\mathrm{J} \gtrsim 3\)) that exhibit cometary features in observations. A major focus of my work has been investigating the mechanisms behind this activity—such as volatile sublimation, rotational instability, or impacts. In an extensive investigation of 18 active asteroids (Hui & Jewitt 2017), we successfully detected nongravitational accelerations in 313P/Gibbs and 324P/La Sagra. These findings revealed that while their mass-loss rates are considerably lower than those of typical comets, the recoil forces from anisotropic mass loss are measurable. This supports the hypothesis that the majority of active asteroids formed in situ within the main belt rather than originating from the outer solar system. My doctoral advisor David Jewitt maintains a special page on this population.


Near-Sun Objects

My fascination with near-Sun small bodies began during high school through the citizen science Sungrazer Project using SOHO and STEREO coronagraphs. These objects, particularly sungrazing comets with perihelion distances \(q \lesssim 5R_\odot\), face extreme tidal and thermal environments. My research focuses on observing their physical behaviors and disruption processes near perihelion. For example, we analysed the disintegration of comet C/2015 D1 (SOHO) (Hui et al. 2015), attributing its demise to soaring interior thermal gradients or rotational instability. More recently, we conducted a multi-telescope campaign on the periodic near-Sun comet 323P/SOHO (Hui et al. 2022), securing its first ground-based recovery. We discovered that 323P undergoes severe thermal fracturing, possesses an anomalous color, and rotates rapidly with a period of just over half an hour. These studies provide crucial insights into why the periodic near-Sun population is so heavily depleted.


Ultradistant Comets

Cometary activity is traditionally expected to be driven by the sublimation of water ice, which typically turns on around heliocentric distances of \(r_\mathrm{H} \lesssim 5\) au. However, an increasing number of comets have been observed to exhibit conspicuous comae at extreme distances well beyond the water-ice sublimation zone on the inbound leg of their trajectories. I am deeply interested in characterizing these ultra-distant comets to understand the mechanisms responsible for their inbound activity. For instance, in our study of comet C/2010 U3 (Boattini) (Hui et al. 2019), we discovered that it sustained continuous activity at an unprecedented distance of \(r_\mathrm{H} \approx 26\) au. This implies that the activity is governed by the sublimation of supervolatiles such as \(\mathrm{CO}\) or \(\mathrm{CO}_2\). Investigating these objects provides a rare window into the pristine volatile compositions of Oort cloud members before they undergo significant solar heating.


Trojans

Trojans are small bodies gravitationally locked around the \(L_4\) or \(L_5\) Lagrange points of the Sun-planet system. The primordial population is particularly scientifically significant because they are presumably the witnesses of planetary migrations in the early solar system. While large swarms of Trojans escort Jupiter and Neptune, Saturnian Trojans have historically remained completely elusive due to severe destabilising perturbations from Jupiter. In 2021, we confirmed 2020 XL5 as the second Trojan of the Earth (Hui et al. 2021 ). However, similar to the first Earth Trojan 2010 TK7, its Trojan status is unstable due to Venusian perturbations. More recently, our study (Hui et al. 2024) identified 2019 UO14 as the first known Trojan of Saturn, librating around the leading \(L_4\) point with a period of \(\sim0.7\) kyr. Our \(N\)-body numerical simulations reveal that this object is not primordial but rather a transient Trojan captured from the chaotic Centaur population roughly 2 kyr ago, and it may return to a horseshoe co-orbital configuration in another millennium. Although it exhibited no active coma, we favour the exciting possibility that it could be the first active Trojan in our solar system.


Interstellar Objects

The discoveries of 1I/`Oumuamua, 2I/Borisov, and 3I/ATLAS opened a new frontier in planetary sciences, offering the first opportunities to study macroscopic material ejected from extrasolar planetary systems. My research in this area focuses on high-precision astrometry and physical characterisation to understand their physical properties. For instance, in a targeted study of the interstellar comet 2I/Borisov ( Hui et al. 2020), we utilised high-cadence ground-based observations to monitor its behaviour. From its nongravitational acceleration, we constrained its nucleus size and erosion due to its cometary activity. In a more recent study of ours (Hui et al. 2026), we used the Hubble Space Telescope to successfully measure the nucleus size of 3I/ATLAS and spotted a significant opposition effect of its cometary dust that does not resemble any of the known solar system comets.
